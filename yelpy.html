A Conversational UI Bot

The Problem

Preliminary research shows that Yelp’s current method of presenting reviews and ratings on partner sites such as BtownMenus is tedious and ineffective. Yelp’s presence itself is diminished and irrelevant to the users in the way in which it is currently incorporated on the site. There is a need to emphasize Yelp’s relevance on the partner site and to make the reviews and ratings more easily accessible and relevant.

Approach

User interviews, affinity diagramming, paper prototyping and usability testing.

Project duration

2 Weeks of total project time. The deliverables included a design document, a presentation deck and an iterations document.

Solution

Yelpy, the food expert is a conversational UI bot that provides food choices to its users based on Yelp’s reviews and ratings. It also leverages machine learning capabilities to study user behaviour and provide personalised recommendations. In this design Yelpy is integrated into BTownMenus, a local food delivery service application based in the college town of Bloomington, Indiana.

The beginning

The design prompt that we were given asked us to explore alternatives to Yelp’s current method of providing ratings and reviews using a conversational UI. Our goal was to improve the experience of the user when they’re looking for something to eat on BTownMenus.com.

BTownMenus is a popular local delivery service in Bloomington, Indiana that links restaurants which particularly appeal to college students with late-night delivery services.

User research

We began by trying to understand how users currently used BTownMenus and whether they found BtownMenus’ current yelp reviews feature to be helpful.

Through our initial research, we gathered that Indiana University’s graduate and undergraduate students made up a huge portion of BTownMenu’s primary users. Our goal was to understand if they ever utilized Yelp reviews, and when they found the need to use them. We conducted scripted interviews with 13 of our users about their experiences, their motivations and their process of using BTownMenus and Yelp reviews. Using a casual conversational tone we asked them questions about their process and decisions that motivated them in ordering their food choices online.

We created mind maps and visually represented our research to make sense of all the data and view clear patterns of use cases within them.

Mind Mapping of our Research Data

Based on our mind map we drew several insights on what the users required and how we can use Yelp’s data to improve their food ordering experience.

  • Most of the users did not use Yelp reviews because they found Yelp tedious to use, or were not aware of its presence on the site.
  • Users would be more likely to trust suggestions from other customers over the restaurant or business’ suggestions. Some users were skeptical about restaurant recommendations thinking they had some sort of business motive.
  • Users care about quality. Seeing a review will give them a first-person perspective on the food experience of a restaurant.
  • The current integration of Yelp’s reviews on BTownMenus is inconspicuous and convoluted. Due to this the relevancy has dropped.

Our goal became very clear. We needed to make Yelp more relevant and noticeable, and we needed do it in a transparent way.

Our primary user research provided a good insight about our target users. We gained an understanding about their background, lifestyle, activities and behaviour. Based on our study, we came up with two personas who represented the full range of our target demographic and drove our design decisions and helped build specific use cases.

Personas - Matt and Megan

Design Ideation

I led the team in an ideation process where we discussed several ways in which we could design the solution. The prompt had given us a very specific constraint to solve this problem - using a conversational UI. We took to the whiteboard and began dishing out several ideas based on the insights we gained from the primary research on our users and the secondary research on creating conversational bots. Our sketches were deliberately half-baked, leaving space for improvements and modifications. At the end of the session, we looked at what each of us had come up with and marked specific design ideas that we all liked. Then we began the process all over again, exploring more possibilities based on our initial round of sketching.

Ideation

Design Concept

We came up with ‘Yelpy’, our food expert who would provide popular reviews through a conversation, while passively studying user behaviour and providing better recommendations over time. He would have to be a non-intrusive bot to avoid overstepping the user boundaries, while still being able to encourage people to have a conversation. Our main focus was to make sure that Yelpy behaves as an effective tool in helping users find and choose better food quicker, and more easily without any limitations.

Storyboard of the User Experience

Storyboard I created to visualise the user interaction

The key factors we explored with our design were:

  • The conversational style in which Yelpy would interact with its users.
  • Where, when and how Yelpy would be integrated into BTownMenus.

Based on our secondary research on effective incorporation of a conversational UI as a tool, we designed Yelpy with very specific capabilities.

  • We generated a variety of ideas for the conversation flow and explored different personality traits for our bot.
  • We gave a specific focus to the language style that Yelpy would use to enable the users to identify and connect comfortably with him.
  • We placed a predetermined delay between each of Yelpy’s responses to make him feel more real to the users.
  • We limited the number of Yelpy’s suggestions within each conversational phase to avoid overwhelming the users and to keep the experience clean.
  • We also constrained Yelpy's recommendations to the current restaurant page that users are viewing unless he runs out of popular recommendations in that restaurant or to support a particular dietary need.
  • Yelpy recognizes when users add food choices from multiple restaurants to their carts, and he provides a heads up to avoid confusion among users.
Yelpy's Interface
Yelpy's use case responses

All conversations with Yelpy are in the form of pre-programmed clickable responses. We made this design choice based on the following factors that we validated through user testing and interviews.

  • Our secondary research suggested that clickable texts are always more accessible than open ended text conversations
  • Pre-determined options allow for faster response time, thus making the conversation more structured
  • Limited size of the mobile screen does not always invite easy texting
  • We designed our interface such that the conversations can always be lead with a simple "yes”, “no” or “what else”

Integration of Yelpy into BTownMenus:

We changed the original layout of BtownMenus’ mobile app to bring more emphasise on Yelpy. We chose to redesign the top navigation bar to remove redundant features and utilize easily recognizable icons.

Yelpy's integration into BTown Menus

We wanted to provide the users with an accurate and condensed summary of the reviews on Yelp through the ease of quick messages. Yelpy also uses machine learning techniques to remember user preferences and recommend better suggestions to the users (i.e. dietary restrictions).

Usability Testing yelpy

We created several low fidelity paper prototypes of Yelpy’s UI interactions, to test the usability of the design with the users.

  • Effectiveness of the language and communication with the users.
  • Efficiency of Yelpy during edge case scenarios such as dietary restrictions and during zero search results.
  • Unobtrusiveness of Yelpy’s pop up feature.
  • User’s responses to receiving recommendations from Yelpy.
  • Ease of use of the chat interface and the experience of its integration within BTownMenus’ app.

User interacting with the paper prototypes

We conducted task based usability tests and used the ‘think out loud’ protocol to test the experience of our design. I operated the interactive elements of the paper prototype while two of my teammates observed the test in progress and took notes. Another teammate video recorded the test. At the end of the test we asked the users several questions regarding their experience using the interface.

We conducted usability tests on a total of 4 students, three of whom were undergraduates aging between 20 and 21 and a Ph. D graduate student who was 25 years old.

We studied the test results, mapped out the design recommendations and made more changes to our design.

What I Learned

  • All the information you would need to completely understand a platform can be gained through its users.
  • There are several approaches and directions that we could take to solve a problem, but the key is to recognize the one idea that has the most potential.
  • It takes dedicated team work and an open mind to testing out ideas iteratively to successfully design an ideal solution.
  • Designing a conversational interface could easily become complex unless you are ready to embrace the constraints and focus on that one main problem that you need your design to solve in the simplest possible way.
